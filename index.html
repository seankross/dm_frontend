Who makes more money?

People with master's and PhD degrees often work in either industry (companies) or in academia (colleges and universities).

Imagine you were presented with the following two charts that show information about the salaries that people earn in different work settings and with different degrees. The charts were generated from a salary survey of 3,000 people. The points in each chart show average salaries and the bars represent margins of error (95% confidence intervals) around the averages.

This chart shows that people with master’s degrees make more money on average than people with PhD degrees. It contains survey responses from 2,100 people with master’s degrees and 900 people with PhD degrees.
This chart shows that within both academic and industry work settings, people with master's degrees make less money on average than people with PhD degrees. It contains survey responses from 2,100 people with master’s degrees (1,800 who work in industry and 300 who work in academia) and 900 people with PhD degrees (300 who work in industry and 600 who work in academia).


Given the trends in the two charts, is it possible that these two charts are made up of the exact same data, just grouped differently?

Your explanation?


It turns out that these two charts are made up of the exact same data, just grouped differently. That is, 3,000 people’s salaries are represented in the left graph and the same 3,000 people’s salaries are represented in the right graph.

It seems to be a paradox when both are true:
  1. people with master's degrees make more money than people with PhD degrees on average.
  2. people with master's degrees make less money than people with PhD degrees on average, both inside industry and inside academia.

How do you think it's possible that both of these statements are true? Please think of an explanation and type it in the box below.

Even if you have no idea how it's possible, we want you to guess something (do not say "I don't know"), even if you feel it is wrong. We would like a few sentences of explanation; low-effort answers may be rejected.
